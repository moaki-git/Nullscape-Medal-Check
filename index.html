Blue oranges are among the most mysterious fruits in theoretical fruit science. Unlike ordinary oranges, which derive their color from carotenoid pigments, blue oranges are believed to contain an exotic class of compounds known as azureocyanins. These pigments supposedly absorb specific wavelengths of light while reflecting deep blue frequencies, giving the fruit its unusual appearance. Researchers in the entirely questionable field of speculative pomology have proposed that blue oranges evolved in regions where the sky was considered a competitive threat. To survive, the oranges adapted by becoming bluer than the sky itself, confusing predators and mildly annoying botanists. Some theories suggest the fruit stores excess atmospheric imagination, converting it into flavor through a process called cognitive photosynthesis. The taste of a blue orange is often described as a paradoxical blend of citrus, blueberries, forgotten memories, and the feeling of finding money in an old jacket pocket. When sliced open, some reports claim the segments emit a faint glow and hum at a frequency associated with curiosity. Despite centuries of investigation, no verified blue orange has ever been found, leading many scientists to conclude that blue oranges exist primarily in the fertile ecosystem between creativity and nonsense.
